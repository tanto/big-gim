Cities geo-smart: does it take an expert? You could think of a Big GIM (Geographic Information Manager)

febbraio 15, 2015

The geo-data are as a warp to weave a city “smart” and the geo-ICT is flooding the smart city ecosystem of a huge amount of applications; the governance of the processes “smart” is based on the cooperation between the players, involved in a network model. Why not include the professional figure of the Geographic Information Manager within the governance bodies of each smart city -before that by law- as a choice of common sense?

We could identify the Geographic Information Manager as an expert in the field, whose main task is the promotion of the quality level and technical expertise growth within the network that is going to build an intelligent community. His commitment is mainly focused on issues related to the capability to benefit of spatial data (spatial enablement), both assumed with respect to the availability of this information and as a skill achieved for their exploitation.

The issues on which a GIM should be competent and for which, therefore, a training support should be provided, should be thoroughly investigated. Here, two are mentioned by the way of example. The application of the concept “spatial enablement” in the process of a Smart City involves the need to measure the degree of adherence to this paradigm, for instance, to evaluate the ability of Public Administrations, citizens and businesses in the use of spatial data to organize their activities and ways of communicating. The knowledge of the strengths and restrictions allow the needed actions to be defined: then, they must be monitored for tracking their implementation, taking action to correct any deviations from the expected results.

Within this theme, a GIM will have to keep under control and propose actions to improve the ability of the INSPIRE directive implementation, in keeping with the INSPIRE Maintenance and Implementation Framework set up by the European Commission. The work of the GIM can ensure that the city -as a “living thing” – learns how to organize geographic information taking into account the costs to generate them and ensure their use whenever needed, to each user, committing himself -and engaging the city- to create sustainable cycles of geographic information.

His work can also have side effects extremely positive. On the one hand, it may generate a strong demand for new skills related to the world of the Geographic Information, thus prompting the definition of specific training programs both in academia and postgraduate. On the other hand, the solutions found by GIMs could act as a driving force, stimulating great opportunities for the Geo-ICT market growth.
